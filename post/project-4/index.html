Specialization: Time Series Analysis and its Models (Classic, Machine, Deep)

By The Electrical Engineer

July 7, 2021

“Time is gold. It is our most valuable asset.”

“Thus, understanding things that involves time will be rewarding, may be as a person or as a business man."

In this post, I will be sharing my two years of search about Time Series, and how we must deal with it.

First, let us define Time Series.

According to Investopedia.com, “A time series is a sequence of data points that occur in successive order over some period of time. This can be contrasted with cross-sectional data, which captures a point-in-time.” (Understanding Time Series, 2021)

In my mind, time series data is just a bunch of data that is marked by dates. Its like an album of pictures with dates at the back — whew, I missed my mom, she used to do that during the time of photo films — Time series data could be regular or irregular which means it could have a consistent time difference between collected data (regularly spaced) and or not (irregular). There are two kinds of time series: stationary and non-stationary, but don’t you worry, there is a way on how you could make a non-stationary time series to stationary and it is called differencing. I will discuss differencing in the next section, for the meantime, let’s define what a stationary time series is,

In Forecasting: Principles and Practice, “A stationary time series is one whose properties do not depend on the time at which the series is observed.” (8.1 Stationarity and differencing | Forecasting: Principles and Practice (2nd ed), 2021)

Commonly, time series data are non-stationary: has trend, seasonaliy and randomness associated to it. Prior to the appreciation of black-box methods, it is near impossible to analyze to analyze time series data except for moving averages and rolling windows models, but, those mentioned models are just approximations and are commonly used when the time series is purely at random, or in other words — “noise."

Univariate, Multivariate, Correlations and Decompositions

There are two types of time series data: univariate and multivariate time series.Univariate time series are two-dimensional time series data that is composed of two labels, one is dates and the other is the single feature data associated with each date. In the same manner, multivariate time series data is a collection of two or more features, allowing complexities but may introduce relationships.

Correlations in time series is slightly different in visuals, but it was still derived using statistics. Here are some examples,

  • ACF means autocorrelation, and it is basically about the relationship between the current variable and its previous self.
  • PACF or partial autocorrelation relates the relationship of the new value and the first ever data introduced. “For me, its like knowing your relationship with your very first ancestor.”
  • There is another correlation that is used in time series analysis called cross correlations functions. More about it through this link, but in simpler terms its about seeing the relationship between two time series.

Decompositions

Decomposition of time series entails considering a series as a composite of level, trend, seasonality, and noise components.

Decomposition is a useful abstract model for thinking about time series in general and for comprehending problems encountered during time series analysis and forecasting.

—According to (MachineLearningMastery, 2017)

It is the process of cleaning, transformation, standardization, averaging and more. It is not limited to almost anything as long at it will help the accuracy and precision of a model. It may also include experimentation, tuning hyperparameters and adding more features. This procedure caters almost 50% of the search-finding the right model.
